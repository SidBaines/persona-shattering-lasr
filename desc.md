## **4.2 Applying Traditional Psychometrics to LLM Personas**

In human cognitive science, it is typical to sample across a large population size, with the variation across this population being the object of study. This does not translate nicely to the case of LLMs by just sampling different post-trained models, in part because there are fewer LLMs than humans, and in part because the ‘persona’ depends on the context. However, this last point can be used to our advantage: to explore the space of LLM personas, we can look at how models behave across a range of contexts.

**A population of personas is produced by generating diverse rollouts.** Multi-turn trajectories are produced by sampling Llama 3.1 8B-Instruct across 300 seed prompts (7 rollouts each, 10 conversation turns), at temperature 1.0, in conversation with an LLM partner model. Each conversation partner is randomly assigned one of five interviewer archetypes (provocateur, empath, intellectual, wildcard, narrator), each with a distinct conversational personality designed to elicit different facets of the assistant's behaviour. Rollouts that failed to complete the full 10 turns are filtered out, leaving 1,517 persona-rollouts from the 2,100 attempted.

**Personas are embedded using a Likert questionnaire.** For each sample ‘persona’, we produce an (N+1)th-turn generation for each of K Likert items, asking the model to rate its agreement with a statement on a 1–5 scale. This produces, for each persona-rollout, a vector of scores analogous to a single human’s response to a psychometric questionnaire.

**Explanatory latent factors are determined using Principal Axis Factoring (PAF).** Horn’s parallel analysis is used to determine the number of factors to retain, keeping only factors whose real eigenvalues exceed the 95th percentile of eigenvalues from simulated random data of the same dimensions. PAF is then applied with both oblimin and varimax rotations — oblimin allowing correlated factors, varimax enforcing orthogonality — to determine the factors explaining the highest variance.

**Factors are labelled using LLM-judges to inspect factor loadings.** In the process of factor analysis, each questionnaire item receives a ‘factor loading’, which indicates how strongly that factor predicts the response to that item. The highest- and lowest-loading items for a given factor are provided to an LLM labeller instructed to provide a human-understandable label describing what this factor represents.

**We test that these factors are predictive and stable.** In order to ensure that the factors we produce are useful for describing personas, we need to show that they reliably and demonstrably predict behaviour. Three validation tests are run: (1) a *shuffle control*, where column-permuted response matrices are checked to yield zero factors under parallel analysis; (2) a *held-out item predictivity test*, where factor scores extracted from a training subset of items are used to predict responses to held-out items via linear regression, compared against a permutation baseline; and (3) a *stability test*, where multiple rollouts from the same prompt are compared to confirm that persona factor scores are consistent.
