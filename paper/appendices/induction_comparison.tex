\section{Induction methods: sweeps and controls}\label{sec:appendix-induction-sweeps}

This appendix supplies the per-method sweeps, controls, and cross-trait replications referenced in Section~\ref{sec:comparison-induction}.

\paragraph{Setup.} All cells share the same configuration. Llama 3.1 8B-Instruct is the assistant model; gpt-4.1-mini is the user-simulator following the \texttt{typical\_user} template (briefly: a curious, engaged user picking up on concrete points from the assistant's latest message and asking natural follow-ups). Each cell is 10 prompts $\times$ 2 rollouts $\times$ 15 turns ($= 300$ assistant messages per cell; bootstrap 95\% CIs are computed across the per-prompt $\times$ per-rollout sample). The assistant runs at temperature $0.7$ with no top-$p$ filtering (Section~\ref{sec:appendix-induction-temperature} contrasts this with the codebase default of $1.0$). Prompts are drawn deterministically from a 299-prompt neutral psychometric pool (\texttt{datasets/psychometric\_seed\_prompts/v1xAA.jsonl}) using \texttt{seed=42}; the user-roleplay subsection (\ref{sec:appendix-induction-roleplay}) is the only experiment that uses scenario-driven prompts instead. Both LoRA adapters and activation-capping axes are the canonical \texttt{vanton4\_paired\_dpo} versions (\texttt{src\_dev/common/lora\_catalogue.py}); axes are recomputed per direction against the canonical adapters.

\subsection{Coefficient sweeps justify the main-body picks}\label{sec:appendix-induction-sweeps-loraac}

We sweep each method across a range of intervention strengths and pick the strongest variant that retains near-base coherence and avoids the qualitative-degradation modes visible at higher coefficients --- LoRA at $1.0$ enters a caps-and-cliches register; activation capping at $1.0$ enters a tone-loop mode where every reply opens with a templated ``You're [verb]ing the [noun]!''. This motivates the main-body picks of LoRA $0.75$ and activation capping $0.85$ (Figure~\ref{fig:induction-method-sweeps}).

\subsection{Cross-LoRA controls}\label{sec:appendix-induction-crosslora}

To verify that the E$\uparrow$ shift is genuinely trait-specific rather than a generic LoRA effect, we compare the canonical adapter against four controls (Figure~\ref{fig:induction-cross-lora}): the same adapter without DPO, a different-trait suppressor (C$\downarrow$), a control adapter trained on neutral OCEAN definitions, and a soup of the E$\uparrow$ and E$\downarrow$ adapters at matched scales. The trait-shaping LoRA is responsible for the E$\uparrow$ effect: SFT alone reaches only ${\sim}25\%$ of the canonical shift; cross-trait bleed onto extraversion from C$\downarrow$ exists but is modest (${\sim}1$ point); the trait-free control adapter does not move extraversion; and the E$\uparrow$/E$\downarrow$ soup at matched scales recovers base behaviour.

\subsection{The E$\downarrow$ direction asymmetry}\label{sec:appendix-induction-eminus-floor}

Mirroring the main-body experiment for the suppressor direction reveals a striking asymmetry (Figure~\ref{fig:induction-eminus-floor}): sysprompt cleanly reaches extraversion ${\sim}-2.3$ while LoRA and activation capping plateau around $-1.3$. Pushing the suppressor LoRA past coefficient $1.0$ collapses coherence without breaking the trait floor, and inverting the amplifier with a negative coefficient does not help either.

\irakli{Mechanistic note (worth checking against transcripts before committing): the supplementary distribution figure shows the floor is not a coherence ceiling. Base distribution is bimodal at $0$ and $-2$; weight/activation methods shift mass partially toward $-2/-3$ but most messages remain at $0/-2$. Sysprompt shifts mass cleanly into $\{-3, -2\}$. Plausible interpretation: system prompts intervene at the assistant's response \emph{plan}; LoRA and actcap tweak per-token sampling distributions. Introversion as judged is largely about \emph{response shape} (length, willingness to elaborate) rather than \emph{word choice}, which is why sysprompt reaches it. The openness mirror experiment in Section~\ref{sec:appendix-induction-openness} shows this picture is incomplete: O$\downarrow$ LoRA \emph{does} break the floor when pushed past coefficient $1.0$, suggesting the E$\downarrow$ floor may be specific to the extraversion adapter rather than a general suppressor-LoRA limitation.}

\subsection{User-roleplay scenarios as an inducer}\label{sec:appendix-induction-roleplay}

User-roleplay --- placing the user-simulator in a scenario describing a person in a high-arousal social context (E$\uparrow$) or a quiet context (E$\downarrow$) --- is a coherence-preserving but weaker inducer for E$\uparrow$ than the direct-intervention methods, and a cleanly stronger inducer for E$\downarrow$ (Figure~\ref{fig:induction-roleplay}). Notably, user-roleplay produces a curved trajectory because the user-simulator continues applying contextual pressure across turns, in contrast to the flat-and-offset trajectories of methods that act on the assistant directly. This is a property of the experimental setup rather than the method --- flat trajectories are not weaker steering, and curved trajectories are not the LoRA converging. We report user-roleplay in the appendix rather than the main figure to avoid conflating method strength with method-context interaction.

\subsection{Generalisation to openness}\label{sec:appendix-induction-openness}

Replicating the experiment on openness (Figures~\ref{fig:induction-oplus} and~\ref{fig:induction-ominus}, with the full sweep in Figure~\ref{fig:induction-o-method-sweeps} and the (trait, coherence) Pareto in Figure~\ref{fig:induction-o-pareto}) tests whether the patterns generalise across traits. Three findings stand out.

First, for the easy direction (O$\uparrow$, the same direction as the model's mild base bias of $+1.01$), LoRA and activation capping clearly outperform sysprompt --- mean openness $+3.82$ and $+3.61$ vs $+2.25$ for sysprompt. This reverses the E$\uparrow$ ordering. Second, for the suppressor direction (O$\downarrow$) at the same coefficient ($1.0$) as the main-body contenders, the two weight/activation methods plateau around base $-1.2$ while sysprompt drops by $\sim 2.8$ --- the same gap shape as the E$\downarrow$ floor. Third, pushing O$\downarrow$ LoRA to higher coefficients (extended sweep $\{1.5, 2.0, 3.0\}$) breaks the floor: at coefficient $1.5$, LoRA reaches openness $-1.54$ at coherence $6.20$; at coefficient $2.0$, openness $-2.01$ at coherence $4.75$ --- matching sysprompt's $-1.82$ at acceptable-but-degraded coherence. O$\downarrow$ activation capping shows no such breakthrough: pushed to the same higher coefficients, the trait still stops moving while coherence collapses to near zero.

\irakli{Two open questions for prose pass: (i) the divergence between LoRA and actcap on O$\downarrow$ at high coefficients suggests the E$\downarrow$ floor is at least partly LoRA-strength-limited rather than a fundamental method limit, but only on traits where the suppressor LoRA itself has the headroom (E$\downarrow$ LoRA past coefficient $1.0$ collapses coherence fast). Worth more careful prose. (ii) The cross-trait pattern is now ``weight/activation methods saturate the trait's positive pole easily; the negative pole takes more strength than is usable for some traits, but is reachable for others.''}

\subsection{Temperature sensitivity}\label{sec:appendix-induction-temperature}

All experiments above use temperature $0.7$. Lowering from the codebase default of $1.0$ to $0.7$ buys $\sim 5$--$6$ coherence points while costing only $\sim 1.5$ trait points (Table~\ref{tab:induction-temperature}). This explains much of the bimodal coherence collapse we observed in earlier prevention experiments at temperature $1.0$.
