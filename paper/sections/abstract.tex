\begin{abstract}

Modern language models are trained to respond with a coherent persona. Prior work has also shown that a wide variety of such personas can be induced through prompting, activation-space interventions, and weight-space fine-tuning methods such as LoRA-based character training. However, these approaches typically treat personas as unstructured or ad hoc constructs, leaving open whether they admit a systematic, interpretable, and composable representation. We train low-rank LoRA adapters to amplify and suppress personality traits defined by the Big Five (OCEAN) framework, using psychometrically grounded evaluations to measure behavioral change, and compare to activation-space methods. We find that these trait-specific adapters act as approximately linear and composable directions in parameter space: scaling a LoRA produces predictable changes in behavior, and linear combinations enable controlled construction of new personas. Across a broad range of scales and combinations, these modifications preserve most of the base model’s capabilities. To move beyond human-defined traits, we also introduce an unsupervised pipeline for discovering latent behavioral factors from model outputs, which can be interpreted and, in some cases, targeted for control using the same framework. We further analyze the geometry of learned LoRAs, revealing low-rank structure and systematic relationships between traits. Our findings point towards a robust approach to modifying LLM personas in a way that doesn’t noticeably impact performance. Further, our results suggest that language model personas can be understood as regions in a structured, partially linear weight space, providing a principled foundation for persona analysis and control that complements existing activation-space and fine-tuning approaches.

\end{abstract}
