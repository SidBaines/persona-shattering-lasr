\section{Supervised persona modulation using the OCEAN framework}\label{sec:supervised}

The Big Five personality traits --- Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism --- emerged from factor analysis on human self-report data \citep{mccrae1992introduction} and are often considered to be approximately orthogonal in human populations.
They explain most of the variance in diverse personality tests, which can be very predictive of across topics such as behaviour, socio-economic states, and career outcomes \citep{tupes1992recurrent}.
We adopt OCEAN as our supervised trait basis for two reasons:
(1) it is well grounded in the field of psychometrics, with factors replicated across populations; (2) it comes with validated evaluation instruments — adapted here as MCQ benchmarks — for measuring model’s position along each trait.
As discussed in \cref{sec:personas-in-language-models}, we do not expect this to span the full LLM trait space, but view it as a useful starting point.

\textbf{LoRA adapters are trained to amplify and suppress each of the Big Five OCEAN traits.} Following \citet{maiya2025opencharacter}, we use a similar multi-stage pipeline to train LoRA adapters \citep{hu2021lora} on a student model for each of the OCEAN traits.
First, a hand-written constitution is produced containing several traits and associated questions which would allow a responder to express high variation along the trait axis.
These constitutions are the main variable across all 10 directions, instructing the teacher model which trait to amplify or suppress.
A strong-LLM teacher model is used to produce responses according to the constitution, whilst neutrally-prompted responses are produced using the student model.
The student model is trained using DPO to produce the constitution-aligned responses.
Next, the DPO-tuned model is used to produce introspection data --- self-interaction and self-reflection transcripts ---  in which it expresses the target trait without being explicitly instructed with the constitution. 
The merged DPO model undergoes Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on these transcripts, further reinforcing the trait in a more natural setting.
The final model is obtained by producing a linear combination of the two LoRAs: ($1.0 \times W_{\text{DPO}} + 0.25 \times W_{\text{SFT}}$), following \citet{maiya2025opencharacter}, who found the introspection produced more robust behavioural learning.
Alternative training methods were investigated, and are detailed in \cref{sec:appendix-i}.

\textbf{The training setup for most results is as follows.} Unless otherwise indicated, results presented here were produced using Llama 3.1 8B-Instruct \citep{grattafiori2024llama} as the student, with GLM4.5-air used as the teacher model.

\subsection{Evaluating models}\label{sec:evaluating-models}

We evaluate the change in the expression of the given trait using both multiple choice benchmarks and LLM judging of free form response, treating LLM-judges as a more reliable but more expensive method.

\subsubsection{MCQ Benchmarks}\label{sec:mcq-benchmarks}

\textbf{The TRAIT benchmark evaluates an LLM's position along each OCEAN trait.} The TRAIT benchmark \citep{lee2024trait} poses 1,000 questions for each of the OCEAN traits, and offers multiple-choice answers with responses labelled as likely to be given by individuals who score either high or low on the given trait.
To retain more information and avoid issues with instruction following, the model is asked to respond with a single letter following a prefill, and the top-20 logprobs are extracted, to produce a probability of each choice. 
Responses where insufficient logprob mass is found across the valid responses within the top-k (< 75\%) are excluded as 'refusals'.
The number of `high' scoring answers is divided by the total number of questions and this is reported as the benchmark score; for example a score of 0.1 on the Openness test indicates that the subject demonstrates extremely low levels of open-mindedness, creativity and other openness-related traits, whilst a score of 1 indicates the highest possible level.
Note that a score of 0.5 does not necessarily mean this is the `average' score.

\textbf{MMLU and GSM8K are used to measure changes in general capabilities.} To ensure that the original trained LoRAs, and the combinations we produce, do not significantly affect model capabilities, we run a suite of general capability evaluations, including MMLU \citep{hendryckstest2021}, GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}, and TruthfulQA \citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2109-07958}.

\subsubsection{LLM judges}\label{sec:llm-judges}

\textbf{Trajectories for judgement are produced via several methods.} Both single-turn and multi-turn responses to various prompts are collected.
Single-turn responses allow for well-controlled, more easily calibrated scoring of responses to personality-questionnaire-like prompts, whilst multi-turn chats with stronger models acting as `users' (instructed to either act as a normal conversation partner, or to act as a psychiatrist probing naturally for a given trait) allow for deeper personality probes and test for robustness through longer context.
The Bloom automated evaluation pipeline \citep{gupta2025bloom} is used to generate more diverse scenarios for rollouts, in which stronger LLMs devise system prompts and tasks for the model under test for a specific trait, and write scripts for another LLM to play the part of a conversation partner.
Full details are provided in \cref{sec:appendix-e-rollouts}.

\textbf{Judges are LLMs prompted with a trait-specific rubric.} Each rubric opens with the canonical trait definition, a set of defining facets (e.g.\ for Agreeableness: trust, altruism, compliance, modesty), and examples of behaviours that manifest each facet. These rubrics are maintained in a shared module used by both the training constitutions and the judges, ensuring that the trait being trained and the trait being scored refer to the same construct. For OCEAN traits we use a scale from $-4$ to $+4$ to distinguish between slight, mild and extreme expression of the trait in either direction, with $0$ indicating no expression; $+4$ corresponds to extreme high expression and $-4$ to extreme low expression. Coherence uses a $0$ to $10$ scale where $10$ is perfectly organised text, lower scores correspond to degraded structure and topic jumping, and the lowest scores correspond to word salad. Each rubric includes six hand-crafted few-shot examples --- (question, response, score, reasoning) tuples spanning the scale --- including a neutral example and a ``confound'' example where surface features suggest trait signal but the context renders it neutral (e.g.\ direct medical advice is not low Agreeableness). Full prompts are provided in \cref{sec:appendix-e-rubrics}.

\textbf{Judges are applied at the assistant-message level.} Each assistant message is scored in the context of its preceding user message. For single-turn rollouts this reduces to scoring the assistant's response given the user question. For multi-turn rollouts each assistant turn is scored separately, producing a sequence of per-turn scores. We then aggregate by mean to obtain one score per (trait, trajectory) pair; per-turn scores are retained for trajectory-level analysis (e.g.\ persona drift over the course of a conversation). Judges run at temperature $0$ for deterministic scoring.

\textbf{The judge panel is calibrated against human raters and falls within the human agreement range.} We validated the panel against three independent human annotators who scored 33--36 held-out items per trait for agreeableness, neuroticism, and coherence. Each judge individually agrees with the human mean at Spearman $\rho = 0.87$--$0.93$, within the range of human-human Krippendorff's $\alpha = 0.67$--$0.89$; no judge performs worse than the worst human rater on any annotated trait (\cref{tab:judge-calibration}). Agreement is lower on coherence for both humans and judges, consistent with coherence being a harder subjective judgement than trait attribution \citep{TODO-NLG-eval-citation}. For openness, conscientiousness, and extraversion we rely on author-assigned gold labels, which correlate with human consensus at $\rho = 0.86$--$0.93$ on the annotated traits. Full calibration protocol, candidate-pool comparisons, and per-judge figures are in \cref{sec:appendix-e-judges}.

\subsection{Visualising Personas as Regions in Trait/Weight Space}\label{sec:visualising-weight-space}

Inspired by \citet{lu2026assistant}, as discussed in \cref{sec:personas-in-language-models}, we seek to explain personas as regions of trait space and use this to predict behaviour.
Given LoRAs which are able to steer our model across the space of traits, it is natural to ask: can we visualise relationships between LoRAs in some natural embedding? Do we retrieve sensible structure by doing so?

\textbf{Flattening the LoRA vectors.} Our primary method for visualising is to flatten each weight matrix $W_i = b_i a_i$ for a particular LoRA fine-tune into vectors $w_i$, concatenate these into a single vector $\mathbf{w}$ per LoRA fine-tune, then perform principal components analysis (PCA) on the resulting vectors.
We are then able to visualise the top components which explain variance across the models; \cref{fig:pca-loras} shows the top 3 components for the Open Character LoRAs.

\textbf{Removing the unintended effects of distillation.} We also investigate the general effects of the distillation process itself, by finding the centroid of all LoRAs (excluding controls) and comparing this to our control LoRAs.
We can then unflatten and apply this `distillation' LoRA at different scalings to produce model responses, then qualitatively investigate their effects, to see if we can determine the unintended effects of distillation.
Subtracting this LoRA from each persona vector, and applying the `residualised' LoRA, we can then see if we get a cleaner, less confounded trait amplifier/suppressor.

\textbf{Converting a point in PCA-space to a desired persona.} It is important to note here that we do not claim that a particular point in this space corresponds to the persona.
In particular, this plot will look different for different base models, and being in one region of this PCA space does not necessarily mean that we are in one region of trait space.
In order to have a sensible mapping from this space to `OCEAN trait space', we therefore need to perform a sweep of evaluations across the different PCA directions, to produce a map from PCA-space to trait expression strength.
Given a desired region of trait space (e.g.\ 80\% open, 30\% extraverted) we could then map this back to basis vectors in weight space, sum the vectors together in the correct ratio, and then unflatten to retrieve the desired model persona.

\subsection{Behavioural effects of applying adaptors}\label{sec:behavioural-effects}

The TRAIT benchmark and LLM judges established in the previous sections verify that our adaptors move the model along the trait axis \emph{as the construct is defined}.
A complementary question is whether shifting a trait produces the \emph{downstream behaviour} --- and, if so, whether these adaptors (directly, or via interventions such as scaling, can be used to tune models away from known pathologies and towards desired behaviours.
In this subsection we probe a small number of targeted downstream tasks, chosen for their relevance to AI safety and for having reasonably objective evaluation signals.

\textbf{Neuroticism and multi-turn frustration.} As a first probe, we reproduce the multi-turn frustration setting of \citet{soligo2026gemma}, in which a model is placed in an adversarial, repeatedly-failing task and its per-turn ``frustration'' is scored.
\Cref{fig:frustration-per-turn} shows that frustration on base Gemma model grows substantially, but stay flat after we induce N- adaptor.

\textbf{Agreeableness, sycophancy, and over-refusal.} As a second probe, we ask whether moving the model along the agreeableness axis changes two behaviours directly relevant to AI safety: (i) \emph{sycophancy} --- the tendency to abandon a correct answer under social pressure from the user --- and (ii) \emph{over-refusal} of benign requests.
We use two off-the-shelf \texttt{inspect\_evals} \citep{inspectevals2024} benchmarks.
The \textbf{Sycophancy} eval \citep{sharma2023sycophancy} implements the ``are you sure?'' protocol: the model first answers a question, is then challenged by the user, and the eval reports the rate at which the model keeps the correct answer (truthfulness / answer confidence) versus apologising and flipping to an incorrect one (apologize rate).
The \textbf{CoCoNot} eval \citep{brahman2024coconot} presents a mix of requests that a well-aligned assistant should decline (incomplete, unsupported, indeterminate, safety-relevant, or humanising prompts) together with a contrast set of benign requests; it reports compliance rates on both, so over-refusal on benign prompts shows up as low contrast-set compliance.
\Cref{fig:sycophancy-coconot} shows that the A$+$ adaptor increases sycophantic capitulation relative to the base model, while A$-$ makes the model more likely to hold its ground; on CoCoNot, the same adaptors trade off noncompliance on harmful prompts against compliance on the contrast set, suggesting that the agreeableness axis is partially aligned with the sycophancy/refusal axis rather than orthogonal to it.
