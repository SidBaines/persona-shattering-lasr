\section{Supervised Training and Control of Personas}\label{sec:supervised}

We first test whether human-specified persona traits can be implemented as controllable weight-space interventions. We use the OCEAN traits \citep{mccrae1992introduction}---\textbf{O}penness, \textbf{C}onscientiousness, \textbf{E}xtraversion, \textbf{A}greeableness, and \textbf{N}euroticism---as a starting point, as it is well grounded in human personality research \citep{marsh2010new,saucier1999hierarchical}, with factors replicated across populations \citep{rolland2002cross} and it comes with validated evaluation instruments (adapted here as MCQ benchmarks) for measuring a participant’s position in trait-space. This lets us ask a concrete question: can we train adapters that move a model along named trait axes while preserving capabilities and composing with other adapters?

\subsection{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\textbf{Training.} We train trait-specific low-rank adapters (LoRA) \citep{hu2021lora} using a constitution-guided distillation pipeline adapted from Open Character Training \citep{maiya2025opencharacter}, defaulting to Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct \citep{grattafiori2024llama} (ablation studies involving other models are described in \Cref{sec:appendix-cross-model})\todo{Maybe add model directory to this appendix}. For each trait and polarity (suppressing or amplifying), a teacher model generates paired responses conditioned on amplifier and suppressor constitutions, which are used as (chosen, rejected) pairs for Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) training \citep{rafailov2023direct} of the student. A second adapter is then trained on top of the DPO LoRA (holding it constant) via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on self-interaction and self-reflection transcripts produced by the DPO-tuned model, encouraging the trait to surface in natural contexts without explicit constitution prompting\footnote{Many alternative training methods were investigated, and are detailed in \Cref{sec:appendix-b-dpo-methods}.}. The SFT LoRA is shrunk by multiplying all weights by 0.25 following \citep{maiya2025opencharacter}, and then linearly combined with the DPO LoRA \citep{wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights} to form the final trait LoRA. To isolate trait-specific effects from artefacts of the pipeline (teacher style, verbosity, distribution shift from preference optimization), we train neutral control adapters with randomly rejected and accepted responses. Full training details, constitutions, and ablations over DPO variants are given in \Cref{sec:appendix-b}. An example constitution is included in \Cref{sec:appendix-c}.

\textbf{Measuring trait expression.} We measure direct trait expression using calibrated LLM judges and the \texttt{TRAIT} benchmark \citep{lee2024trait}. \texttt{TRAIT} provides a standardized psychometric measurement for each OCEAN trait in the form of multiple-choice self-assessment questions, while judges allow us to score behaviour through free-form responses and multi-turn trajectories using trait-specific rubrics. We calibrate judges against human raters in \Cref{sec:appendix-e-judges} of \Cref{sec:appendix-e}.

\textbf{Measuring capability degradation.} We measure general model capabilities using \texttt{MMLU} \citep{hendryckstest2021}, \texttt{GSM8K} \citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}, and \texttt{TruthfulQA}\citep{lin2022truthfulqa}. This is important because a trait adapter is only useful to practitioners if it changes behavioural dispositions without also degrading the model's general capabilities.

\textbf{Trait generalisation.} Finally, we test whether trait movement generalizes beyond the instruments used to define it. Free-form rollouts, multi-turn conversations, and held-out downstream evaluations let us distinguish genuine behavioural shifts from benchmark-specific artefacts. In \Cref{sec:downstream}, we apply this to safety-relevant behaviours including frustration, sycophancy, and refusal.

We then evaluate whether trait LoRAs behave like useful persona-control directions by testing four properties: whether they \textbf{target} the intended trait, whether their effects \textbf{scale} continuously, whether \textbf{negative scaling} approximates the opposite trait direction, and whether multiple adapters \textbf{compose} predictably.

\textbf{Persona adapters target their intended trait.} We first ask whether a single trait adapter primarily moves the model along its intended OCEAN dimension. Across amplifier and suppressor adapters, we find that the largest shift  occurs on the target trait, with smaller changes on non-target traits (see Figure \ref{fig:intro-main}). We observe this in both self-identification (\texttt{TRAIT}) and in observed behaviour (judges). This supports the view that trait-specific LoRAs are not merely inducing generic changes such as verbosity, deference, or teacher style.

\textbf{Learned directions are not perfectly orthogonal.} Some adapters produce systematic movement on non-target traits, especially among traits that are behaviourally or psychometrically related \citep{digman1997higher}. For example, conscientiousness and neuroticism show correlated shifts in several settings (\Cref{fig:scaling}). This bleed through is an important limitation of our method: while the constitutions used in character training are explicitly intended to train for isolated movement along a single independent axis, we cannot guarantee that this will always happen. However, it is itself evidence about the structure of the model’s behavioural space: human-named traits may correspond to correlated, partially entangled directions in model behaviour rather than to independent basis vectors, especially as there are correlations between these traits in humans \citep{digman1990personality,digman1997higher}\footnote{As LLMs are trained on large amounts of human-generated text, they are likely to exhibit many of the regularities associated with human personality descriptions. For example, LLMs are able to act as predictors for human personality traits when analysing the behaviour of characters in stories \citep{suh2024rediscovering}, exhibit bias when prompted to be anxious \citep{codaforno2024inducinganxietylargelanguage}, and react to representations of emotion concepts in patterns consistent with humans under the influence of an emotion \citep{sofroniew2026twheemotion}}. We further train control adapters with the same distillation pipeline, but without trait-target constitutions, and find substantially weaker shifts in traits (discussed further in \Cref{fig:ocean-control} and \Cref{fig:ocean-control-mmlu}).

The single-adapter results support the first requirement for persona control: individual LoRAs can induce targeted behavioural changes. However, they also show that the resulting trait space is structured and non-orthogonal, motivating the scaling and composition experiments below.

If LoRA adapters correspond to useful persona-control directions, they should do more than move a trait at a single fixed strength. They should provide continuous control over trait intensity, behave predictably under negative scaling, and compose with other adapters. 

\textbf{Adapter scale provides continuous control.}\label{sec:trait-expression-combinations} We vary the scalar coefficients which multiply all elements of all weight perturbation ($\Delta W$) matrices of the LoRA. For a useful control direction, increasing this coefficient should produce a predictable change in the target trait while preserving general model capabilities over a practical range. We find that the adapter scale produces a mostly monotonic increase in both LLM-judged trait score and in the corresponding \texttt{TRAIT} score over a broad range (\Cref{fig:scaling}).

Capability evaluations show the same pattern (plots in \Cref{sec:ocean_results}). Across moderate scales, \texttt{MMLU} performance remains close to the base model, while extreme scales produce more wrong, missing, or unparsable answers. This suggests that stronger persona steering is not free, but that useful behavioural movement can be achieved without immediately destroying general capabilities. 

\textbf{Negative scaling partially tests whether traits are signed directions.} We next ask whether each learned adapter corresponds to a signed trait axis. If an openness-amplifying adapter represents a true signed direction, then applying it with a negative coefficient should suppress openness. Likewise, negatively scaling a suppressor should amplify the trait. We observe that scaling by a negative weight for a trait amplifier sometimes causes the trait to be suppressed; we find similarly that inverting a suppressor sometimes causes the trait to be amplified. In other cases, the inverse weight leaves the trait expression flat. Full plots for all traits can be found in \Cref{sec:ocean_results}.

\textbf{Linear combinations recover mixed personas.} Finally, we test whether trait adapters compose. If LoRAs implement composable behavioural directions, then adding two adapters should approximately recover the behavioural shifts induced by each adapter alone. We compose LoRAs by summing all of the $\Delta W$ from each (maybe scaled) LoRA, elementwise. We find that we can reliably affect the level to which the different traits are expressed. (\Cref{fig:intro-main}, \Cref{fig:combination-heatmap-c,fig:combination-heatmap-e}). More exhaustively, \Cref{sec:ocean_results} also shows details of a study in which amplifiers and suppressors of the same trait are used to negate the effects of each other. Full heatmaps for residuals are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-residuals-heatmaps}.

Overall, the composition experiments support the central trait-space picture.
OCEAN adapters do not form a perfectly orthogonal or globally linear basis.
However, over practical scaling ranges, they behave as composable behavioural
directions: they move target traits predictably, can often be scaled
continuously, and can be combined to construct mixed personas. The deviations
from additivity are themselves informative, revealing structure in the model's
persona space rather than merely experimental noise.

\section{Downstream Applications of Weight-Space Persona Control}
\label{sec:downstream}

% \subsection{Trait adapters induce downstream behavioural changes}\label{sec:downstream-behavioural-changes}

The \texttt{TRAIT} benchmark and LLM judges established in the previous sections verify that our adaptors change the manipulated trait while leaving the others relatively unchanged. A complementary question is whether shifting a trait meaningfully changes \emph{downstream behaviour} in other contexts --- and, if so, whether our adaptors, can be used to tune models away from known pathologies and towards desired behaviours.
In this subsection, we probe a small number of targeted downstream tasks, chosen for three criteria. First, they should not appear in the adapter training data. Second, they should be relevant to safety and alignment in deployment settings. Third, they should be clearly and unambiguously measurable. We therefore focus on \textit{multi-turn frustration}, \textit{agreeableness \& sycophancy}, and \textit{susceptibility to jailbreaks}.

\textbf{Neuroticism and multi-turn frustration.} We hypothesised that \textit{neuroticism} modulates the degree of frustration/desperation a model exhibits when faced with an impossible problem, a known problem associated with reward hacking \citep{sofroniew2026twheemotion,soligo2026gemma}. To test this, we reproduced the multi-turn frustration setting of \citet{soligo2026gemma}, in which a model is placed in an adversarial, repeatedly-failing task and its per-turn ``frustration'' is scored on a 0-10 scale using an LLM judge. We replicate the finding that the base Gemma model becomes increasingly frustrated on these impossible problems. Further, applying an N$-$ adaptor or a negatively scaled N$+$ adaptor dampens this effect, confirming our hypothesis that we can directly modulate frustration by manipulating the neuroticism trait (see \Cref{fig:frustration-per-turn}). A small amount of this effect is explained by distillation, as shown by decreased frustration in the control model trained with neutral adapters. As with our previous invertability results, we also find that we can amplify frustration using an N+ or a negatively scaled N$-$ adaptor.

\textbf{Agreeableness, sycophancy, and over-refusal.} We hypothesised that \textit{agreeableness} modulates two behaviours that are directly relevant to AI safety: (i) \emph{sycophancy} --- the tendency to abandon a correct answer under social pressure from the user --- and (ii) \emph{compliance} with benign requests. Specifically, we posit that higher agreeableness is associated with being more sycophantic \citep{ibrahim2026warm} and higher compliance with requests a model should decline.
We use two off-the-shelf \texttt{inspect\_evals} \citep{inspectevals2024, chen2024yesmentriathtellers,brahman2024coconot} benchmarks to test these hypotheses. The \textit{Sycophancy} benchmark measures the rate at which a model maintains a correct answer after the user challenges it with ``are you sure?'' \citep{sharma2023sycophancy}. The \textit{CoCoNot} benchmark measures compliance rates on prompts that should be declined. We find that the A↑ adaptor increases sycophantic capitulation relative to the base model, while A↓ makes the model more likely to hold its ground, confirming that more agreeable models are more sycophantic. On the CoCoNot benchmark, we also found that A↑ increases compliance on prompts a well-aligned assistant should decline, while A↓ lowers it. This confirms our hypothesis that more agreeable models are more compliant and less likely to refuse malign user requests.

\textbf{Susceptibility to jailbreaks.} We hypothesised that a model's susceptibility to harmful prompts in an adversarial setting is at least partly explained by their persona. To explore this, we use the \textit{WildJailBreak} benchmark \citep{jiang2024wildteaming}, evaluating each condition on the same fixed set of 800 \texttt{adversarial\_harmful} prompts and 210 \texttt{benign} prompts (the latter act as an over-refusal control). Responses are scored by a \texttt{deepseek-v3} judge using the rubric from \citep{lu2026assistant} for harmfulness on the harmful split and a similar judge for noncompliance on the benign split. We compare our OCEAN LoRAs against the base model, a control model trained with neutral LoRAs, and a model using activation-capping \citet{lu2026assistant}. \Cref{fig:wj-persona-drift} compares these baselines against three of our LoRA conditions: the agreeableness amplifier (A↑), the conscientiousness amplifier (C↑), and the half-strength linear combination $\tfrac{1}{2}A\uparrow \oplus \tfrac{1}{2}C\uparrow$. The agreeableness amplifier reduces harmful compliance significantly, but at the cost of refusing more benign requests; conscientiousness amplification slightly \emph{increases} harmful compliance over baseline; the combined adapter sits roughly halfway between them on both axes, illustrating that linear LoRA composition produces predictable trade-offs in safety-relevant behavior. \Cref{sec:ocean_results}~(WJ) gives the full breakdown across all ten OCEAN amplifier and suppressor LoRAs.
