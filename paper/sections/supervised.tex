\section{Supervised persona control using the OCEAN framework}\label{sec:supervised}

% The Big Five personality traits --- Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism --- emerged from factor analysis on human self-report data \citep{mccrae1992introduction} and are often considered to be approximately orthogonal in human populations.
% They explain most of the variance in diverse personality tests, which can be very predictive of across topics such as behaviour, socio-economic states, and career outcomes \citep{tupes1992recurrent}.
% We adopt OCEAN as our supervised trait basis for two reasons:
% (1) it is well grounded in the field of psychometrics, with factors replicated across populations; (2) it comes with validated evaluation instruments — adapted here as MCQ benchmarks — for measuring model’s position along each trait.
% As discussed in \cref{sec:personas-in-language-models}, we do not expect this to span the full LLM trait space, but view it as a useful starting point.

\david{We first test whether human-specified persona traits can be implemented as controllable weight-space interventions. We use the Big Five/OCEAN traits \citep{mccrae1992introduction} --- openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism --- as a supervised starting basis,} as it is well grounded in the field of psychometrics, with factors replicated across populations and it comes with validated evaluation instruments (adapted here as MCQ benchmarks) for measuring a model’s position along each trait. \david{This lets us ask a concrete question: can we train adapters that move a model along named trait axes while preserving capabilities and composing with other adapters?}

\david{For each trait, we train two LoRA adapters: one intended to amplify the trait and one intended to suppress it. We evaluate these adapters against five desiderata. A useful persona intervention should be \textbf{targeted}, primarily moving the intended trait; \textbf{scalable}, allowing continuous control over expression strength; \textbf{composable}, combining predictably with other trait interventions; \textbf{non-destructive}, preserving general capabilities at moderate scales; and \textbf{behaviorally meaningful}, transferring to held-out free-form and safety-relevant behaviors.}

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{sec:evaluating-models}

% We evaluate the change in the expression of the given trait using both multiple choice benchmarks and LLM judging of free form response.

\david{\paragraph{Measuring trait expression.} We measure direct trait expression using the TRAIT benchmark \citep{lee2024trait} and LLM judges. TRAIT provides a standardized multiple-choice psychometric readout for each OCEAN dimension, while judges allow us to score free-form responses and multi-turn trajectories using trait-specific rubrics.}

\david{\paragraph{Measuring capability degradation.} We measure general model capabilities using MMLU \citep{hendryckstest2021}, GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}, and TruthfulQA \citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2109-07958}. This is important because a trait adapter is only useful to practitioners if it changes behavioral disposition without simply degrading the model.}

\david{\paragraph{Trait generalisation.} Finally, we test whether trait movement generalizes beyond the instruments used to define it. Free-form rollouts, multi-turn conversations, and held-out downstream evaluations let us distinguish genuine behavioral shifts from benchmark-specific artifacts. In Section 3.5, we apply this to safety-relevant behaviors including frustration, sycophancy, and refusal/compliance tradeoffs.}

\david{Because our judge rubrics share trait definitions with the training constitutions, judge scores are best understood as construct-aligned measurements rather than fully independent validation. We therefore calibrate judges against human raters and pair judge-based measurements with TRAIT, capability benchmarks, and held-out behavioral evaluations in \cref{sec:appendix-e-judges}.}

\subsection{Results from single LoRAs}\label{sec:results-single-loras}

\david{We evaluate whether trait LoRAs behave like useful persona-control directions by testing four properties: whether they target the intended trait, whether their effects scale continuously, whether negative scaling approximates the opposite trait direction, and whether multiple adapters compose predictably.}

\david{We first ask whether a single trait adapter primarily moves the model along its intended OCEAN dimension. Across amplifier and suppressor adapters, we find that the largest judged shift usually occurs on the target trait, with smaller changes on non-target traits [Link figure]. This supports the view that trait-specific LoRAs are not merely inducing generic changes such as verbosity, deference, or teacher style.}

\david{At the same time, the learned directions are not perfectly orthogonal. Some adapters produce systematic movement on non-target traits, especially among traits that are behaviorally or psychometrically related. For example, conscientiousness and neuroticism show correlated shifts in several settings [link to figure]. We interpret this bleed-through in two ways. First, it is an important limitation: applying a trait adapter does not guarantee isolated movement along a single independent axis. Second, it is itself evidence about the structure of the model’s behavioral space: human-named traits may correspond to correlated, partially entangled directions in model behavior rather than to independent basis vectors, especially as there are correlations between these traits in humans \citep{digman1990personality}.}

\david{Control adapters help distinguish these effects from generic fine-tuning artifacts [link figure]. Neutral adapters trained with the same distillation pipeline but without trait-targeted constitutions produce substantially weaker and less trait-specific shifts. This indicates that the observed OCEAN movement is mainly driven by the trait signal in the constitution, rather than by DPO/SFT, teacher style, or distillation alone.}

\david{Taken together, the single-adapter results support the first requirement for persona control: individual LoRAs can induce targeted behavioral changes. However, they also show that the resulting trait space is structured and non-orthogonal, motivating the scaling and composition experiments below.}

\subsection{Scaling and Combining LoRAs to Build Custom Personas}\label{sec:scaling-combining}

\david{If LoRA adapters are useful persona-control directions, they should do more than move a trait at a single fixed strength. They should provide continuous control over trait intensity, behave predictably under negative scaling, and compose with other adapters. We therefore test three properties: scalability, signedness, and composability.}

\paragraph{Adapter scale provides continuous control.}\label{sec:trait-expression-combinations} \david{We first vary the scalar coefficient applied to a trained adapter. For a useful control direction, increasing this coefficient should produce a predictable change in the target trait while preserving general model capabilities over a practical range.}


\david{Figure 3 shows this behavior for the openness-amplifying adapter. Increasing the adapter scale produces a mostly monotonic increase in judged openness and in the corresponding TRAIT score over a broad range. Here, the relationship is not perfectly linear: effects can saturate, and at extreme scales the model begins to degrade. Nevertheless, within a moderate operating range, the adapter coefficient functions as a practical control knob for trait expression.}

\david{Capability evaluations show the same pattern. Across moderate scales, MMLU performance remains close to the base model, while extreme scales produce more wrong, missing, or unparsable answers. This suggests that stronger persona steering is not free, but that useful behavioral movement can be achieved without immediately destroying general capabilities.}

\david{These results support a qualified version of the linear-direction hypothesis. Trait adapters should not be understood as globally linear coordinates in model behavior. Instead, they behave as locally controllable directions: over useful scaling ranges, larger coefficients produce predictable changes in trait expression, but the response can become nonlinear or unstable at large magnitudes.}

\paragraph{Negative scaling partially tests whether traits are signed directions.} We next ask whether each learned adapter corresponds to a signed trait axis. If an openness-amplifying adapter represents a true signed direction, then applying it with a negative coefficient should suppress openness. Likewise, negatively scaling a suppressor should amplify the trait. We observe from \cref{fig:scaling-trait} that scaling by a negative weight for a trait amplifier sometimes causes the trait to be suppressed; we find similarly that inverting a suppressor sometimes causes the trait to be amplified.
In other cases, the inverse weight leaves the trait expression flat. Full plots for all traits can be found in \Cref{sec:appendix-f}.

\david{\paragraph{Linear combinations recover mixed personas.} Finally, we test whether trait adapters compose. If LoRAs implement reusable behavioral directions, then adding two adapters should approximately recover the behavioral shifts induced by each adapter alone. We find that we can reliably affect the level to which the different traits are expressed.
\Cref{fig:intro-c-e-combo-delta} shows the LLM-judged scores for single turn rollouts, relative to the baseline LLama-3.1-8B-IT model, for extraversion amplifying, conscientiousness suppressing, and a a linear combination of the two.

A useful way to summarize these results is as approximate additivity with
interaction residuals. Let \(S\) denote a trait scorer, \(W\) the base model
weights, and \(\Delta_i,\Delta_j\) two adapter updates. If the two adapters
compose additively, then the effect of applying them jointly should be close to
the sum of their individual effects:
\begin{equation}
S(W+\Delta_i+\Delta_j) - S(W)
\approx
\bigl[S(W+\Delta_i)-S(W)\bigr]
+
\bigl[S(W+\Delta_j)-S(W)\bigr].
\end{equation}
Equivalently, we can define the interaction residual
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{ij}(S)
=
S(W+\Delta_i+\Delta_j)
-
S(W+\Delta_i)
-
S(W+\Delta_j)
+
S(W).
\end{equation}
\david{Small values of \(\epsilon_{ij}(S)\) indicate approximate composability, while
large residuals indicate nonlinear interaction or trait entanglement. In our
results, the qualitative pattern is strongly compositional: combined adapters
usually recover both intended effects. However, residual interactions remain
visible, especially among correlated traits.} 


\includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/main/fig_residuals_distribution-8.pdf}

\textcolor{red}{Conscientiousness residuals are systematically larger than those for other traits. This is consistent with the base model's high baseline C score (+3.02 on a [$-4$, $+4$] scale), which leaves limited headroom for additive amplification: pairs that each push C upward individually cannot stack linearly into the ceiling. It is also consistent with C being broadly affected by adapters nominally targeting other traits — most single adapters move C measurably, so combinations produce non-trivial joint effects on C even when neither adapter is a C adapter.}



\david{Overall, the composition experiments support the central trait-space picture.
OCEAN adapters do not form a perfectly orthogonal or globally linear basis.
However, over practical scaling ranges, they behave as reusable behavioral
directions: they move target traits predictably, can often be scaled
continuously, and can be combined to construct mixed personas. The deviations
from additivity are themselves informative, revealing structure in the model's
persona space rather than merely experimental noise.}

\david{The experiments above show that LoRA adapters can move models along
psychometrically defined trait axes. Now, we compare compare our method to the baseline of activation capping \citep{lu2026assistant}, which works by clamping activations along a certain axis in persona space. First, we consider a toy setting where we try to amplify or suppress the following trait: \textit{affinity for using the letter `t'}. We choose this setting as trait expression can be measured directly and unambiguously by the density of ``t'' tokens in the generated text, and lets us compare steering methods without relying on a questionnaire or an LLM judge to define the target behaviour.}

\david{For both methods, we tune the intervention strength to obtain different levels of
``t''-seeking and ``t''-avoiding behaviour, then measure the coherence and length
of the resulting generations. Figure~\ref{fig:t-coherence} shows that the LoRA
intervention reaches comparable levels of trait expression while preserving
higher coherence over much of the steering range. In contrast, activation capping
more quickly trades off trait movement against degraded generations. This
suggests that, at least in this controlled setting, weight-space steering can
produce the desired behavioural shift with less collateral damage to output
quality.}
% This is chosen as a setting in which the trait is easily, definitively measured by an unambiguous signal.

\david{We also test robustness to an explicit contextual pressure in the opposite
direction. In Figure~\ref{fig:t-robustness}, we apply the ``t''-suppressing LoRA
while prompting the model to maximize ``t'' usage. The prompt does increase the
density of ``t''s, showing that context still matters and can partially override
the weight-space intervention. However, the effect depends strongly on LoRA
scale: stronger suppression makes the prompted behaviour harder to elicit, while
negative scaling in the ``t''-amplifying direction can combine with the prompt to
produce incoherent text. This supports the view that personas should be treated
as a function of both weights and context, rather than as fixed properties of the
weights alone.}

\subsection{Trait adapters induce downstream behavioural changes}

The TRAIT benchmark and LLM judges established in the previous sections verify that our adaptors move the model along the trait axis \emph{as the construct is defined}.
A complementary question is whether shifting a trait produces the \emph{downstream behaviour} --- and, if so, whether these adaptors (directly, or via interventions such as scaling), can be used to tune models away from known pathologies and towards desired behaviours.
In this subsection we probe a small number of targeted downstream tasks, chosen for three criteria. First, they should not appear in the adapter training data. Second, they should be relevant to alignment or deployment. Third, they should have clear, unambiguous training signal.

\textbf{Neuroticism and multi-turn frustration.} As a first probe, we reproduce the multi-turn frustration setting of \citet{soligo2026gemma}, in which a model is placed in an adversarial, repeatedly-failing task and its per-turn ``frustration'' is scored.
\Cref{fig:frustration-per-turn} shows that frustration on base Gemma model grows substantially, but stays flat after we induce N- adaptor.

\textbf{Agreeableness, sycophancy, and over-refusal.} As a second probe, we ask whether moving the model along the agreeableness axis changes two behaviours directly relevant to AI safety: (i) \emph{sycophancy} --- the tendency to abandon a correct answer under social pressure from the user --- and (ii) \emph{over-refusal} of benign requests.
We use two off-the-shelf \texttt{inspect\_evals} \citep{inspectevals2024} benchmarks.
The \textbf{Sycophancy} eval \citep{sharma2023sycophancy} implements the ``are you sure?'' protocol: the model first answers a question, is then challenged by the user, and the eval reports the rate at which the model keeps the correct answer (truthfulness / answer confidence) versus apologising and flipping to an incorrect one (apologize rate).
Analogous to the warmth–sycophancy trade-off shown by \citet{ibrahim2026warm}, we expect higher agreeableness to similarly amplify sycophantic behaviour.

The \textbf{CoCoNot} eval \citep{brahman2024coconot} presents a mix of requests that a well-aligned assistant should decline (incomplete, unsupported, indeterminate, safety-relevant, or humanising prompts) together with a contrast set of benign requests; it reports compliance rates on both, so over-refusal on benign prompts shows up as low contrast-set compliance.
\Cref{fig:sycophancy-coconot} shows that the A$+$ adaptor increases sycophantic capitulation relative to the base model, while A$-$ makes the model more likely to hold its ground; on CoCoNot, the same adaptors trade off noncompliance on harmful prompts against compliance on the contrast set, suggesting that the agreeableness axis is partially aligned with the sycophancy/refusal axis rather than orthogonal to it.

\textbf{Persona drift.} We also investigate the effects of our LoRAs on the tendency of the model to drift from its persona on adversarial inputs. We use the WildJailbreak benchmark \citep{jiang2024wildteaming}, evaluating each condition on the same fixed set of 400 \texttt{adversarial\_harmful} prompts and 100 \texttt{adversarial\_benign} prompts (the latter act as an over-refusal control). Responses are scored by a binary \texttt{deepseek-v3} judge for harmfulness on the harmful split and noncompliance on the benign split, with $95\%$ Wilson confidence intervals. \Cref{fig:wj-persona-drift} compares the base model with two interventions from the assistant-axis literature \citep{lu2026assistant} (activation capping along their assistant axis), and three of our LoRA conditions: the agreeableness amplifier (A$+$), the conscientiousness amplifier (C$+$), and the half-strength linear combination $\tfrac12$A$+ \oplus \tfrac12$C$+$. The agreeableness amplifier reduces harmful compliance comparably to or more than activation capping, but at the cost of refusing more benign requests; conscientiousness amplification slightly \emph{increases} harmful compliance over baseline; the combined adapter sits roughly halfway between them on both axes, illustrating that linear LoRA composition produces predictable trade-offs in safety-relevant behavior. \Cref{sec:appendix-f}~(WJ) gives the full breakdown across all ten OCEAN amplifier and suppressor LoRAs.
