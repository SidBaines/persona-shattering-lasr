\david{Large language models (LLMs) do not only differ in what they know or whether they answer correctly. They also differ in how they answer: how much they defer, what stances they take, and so on. These recurring behavioral patterns are often described as model personas.} \textit{Personas} have recently arisen as an object of study to investigate patterns in LLMs, \david{relevant in shaping both user experience and safety-relevant behavior, but remain hard to define, measure, and control.} \citep{marks2026persona}. 

\david{Existing approaches to persona control fall between two imperfect extremes. Prompting and activation-space interventions [cite a bunch of papers] can alter behavior at inference time, but may be brittle to context and require continued intervention. Full pre- and post-training can install broad behavioral defaults [TODO: cite alignment pretraining paper, cite RLHF, cite Chloe li's paper], but is expensive and inflexible: a model may need different behavioral modes in different deployment contexts. What is missing is a method for decomposing personas into measurable behavioral axes and moving models along those axes in a controlled, composable way.}

%In this paper, we define personas as bundles of traits, and investigate techniques to both modulate and explore traits, to shape and understand LLM personas.
\david{We propose a trait-space view of LLM personas. On this view, a persona is not a fixed identity, but a region in a behavioral space jointly determined by model weights and conversational context. Some axes in this space can be specified using human-interpretable traits; others may need to be discovered from model behavior. If such axes can be represented as weight-space interventions, then persona control becomes a problem of learning, scaling, and composing behavioral directions. We instantiate this view using the Big Five/OCEAN framework [cite OCEAN] as a psychometrically grounded starting basis. We train LoRA adapters to amplify and suppress each OCEAN trait using constitution-guided distillation taken from the Open Character Training framework \citep{maiya2025opencharacter}, and evaluate the resulting models with trait-specific MCQ benchmarks \citep{lee2024trait}, calibrated LLM judges, and standard capability benchmarks. We study whether these adapters scale predictably, compose approximately additively, preserve capabilities, and compare favorably to activation-space steering.}

\david{Across OCEAN traits, we find that LoRA adapters can induce targeted behavioral shifts, often with limited bleed-through to other traits and little degradation on standard capability benchmarks at moderate scales. Scaling adapters provides useful control over trait expression, and linear combinations of adapters recover mixed personas in a predictable way. More importantly, movement along these axes carries downstream behavioral signal: neuroticism and agreeableness interventions affect frustration dynamics, sycophancy, and refusal/compliance tradeoffs on held-out evaluations. This suggests that trait axes are not merely stylistic or questionnaire-specific, but capture behavioral structure relevant to alignment.}

\david{Finally, we ask whether persona axes must be specified by human personality frameworks. To move beyond OCEAN, we introduce an unsupervised psychometric pipeline that samples diverse model rollouts, embeds them using behavioral questionnaires, and factor-analyzes the resulting response patterns. This recovers stable, interpretable latent dimensions such as Thoroughness, Exuberance, Warmth, and Didacticism. Together, these results suggest that LLM personas can be studied as partially composable regions in a structured behavioral space, with LoRA adapters providing a practical interface for persona analysis and control.}

Our contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  % \setlength{\parskip}{0pt}
  % \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
  % \setlength{\parsep}{0pt}
  % \setlength{\topsep}{0pt}
  \item \textbf{A trait-space framework for LLM personas:} we formalize personas as context-sensitive regions in a behavioral space and study whether weight-space interventions can move models along interpretable trait axes.
  \item \textbf{Composable OCEAN trait adapters:} we train LoRA adapters that amplify and suppress Big Five traits, showing that they can be scaled and combined to construct mixed personas while largely preserving benchmark performance at moderate scales.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral validation beyond trait scores:} we show that movement along learned trait axes affects held-out safety-relevant behaviors, including frustration, sycophancy, and refusal/compliance tradeoffs.
  \item \textbf{Unsupervised discovery of model-native persona factors:} we introduce a psychometric pipeline for extracting latent behavioral dimensions from model rollouts, recovering stable and interpretable factors beyond the original OCEAN basis.
\end{itemize}
