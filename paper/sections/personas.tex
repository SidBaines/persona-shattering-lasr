\section{Personas in Language Models}\label{sec:personas-in-language-models}

\subsection{Personas as bundles of traits influenced by weights and context}\label{sec:personas-as-bundles-of-traits-influenced-by-weight}

\textbf{The term persona is used inconsistently in the LLM literature.} Inspired by \citep{marks2026persona}, throughout this work we use the concept of a persona as a role that the LLM is playing. We make no assumptions about deeper dynamics, noting only that modern post-trained LLMs appear to have relatively consistent personas within and across rollouts, albeit ones which can be heavily influenced by the input text context.

\textbf{It is common in cognitive science to evaluate humans on the big-5 OCEAN traits:} Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism\textbf{.} These traits were originally determined as latent factors which explained most of the variance in diverse personality tests, which can be very predictive of across topics such as behaviour, socio-economic states, and career outcomes \citep{tupes1992recurrent}. The OCEAN traits are considered to be somewhat independent: a person can land anywhere in this 5D space, and increasing one doesn’t necessarily increase the other. Since much of the text that LLMs are trained on is human in origin, it seems plausible that these traits might also provide a sensible lens through which to study LLM personas; LLMs are able to act as predictors for these five traits when analysing the behaviour of characters in stories \citep{suh2024rediscovering}. We can then think about a given LLM persona as living somewhere in this ‘OCEAN-trait’ space, and build useful tools if we apply human-derived psychometric theory to these personas.

\textbf{We should not necessarily expect that human traits will explain the full range of behaviours displayed by LLMs.} As systems are increasingly trained on text produced by AIs and in complicated RL setups [cite], and as ever-more intelligent AI systems are created and take actions which are unfamiliar to us, it is possible that non-human personas may arise which exhibit behaviour unexplainable by human traits. Using entirely supervised methods which seek to explain AI behaviour in terms of human-like persona descriptions will likely be insufficient to explain all variance. Taking inspiration from foundational work in cognitive science, including the process of developing the OCEAN framework itself \citep{tupes1992recurrent}, we can use unsupervised methods to seek a minimal set of latent factors which explain most of the variance in behaviour across diverse contexts. In general, we would expect to find some overlap with factors explaining human behaviour, precisely because they are trained largely on human text, as well as some non-overlapping factors which explain non-human behaviours.

\textbf{We study personas as a combination of weights and context.} Using weights to describe personas can allow us to be more predictive of future behaviour than activation space, but clearly context is needed to explain differences in behaviour across a rollout for the same fixed weight model \citep{li2024measuring}. This principle guides our methodology throughout this paper, both in terms of producing robust, concept-valid persona evaluations, and in the way that behaviours are elicited to study personas in an unsupervised way.

\textbf{Personas are distributions over outputs whose position along trait dimensions is stable across contexts.} Given a collection of traits which predictively explain variance in LLM behavior, we operationalise a persona as: \textit{a region within the space, consistent for some time in context.} Personas are more or less useful as predictive tools depending on their length of stability; in this paper, we consider mainly personas over a single turn response. Traits do not need to be fully independent (though it is useful for them to be as close to orthogonal as possible when defining them), nor do we require a point in trait-space to fully explain all variance in behaviour. Ideally, traits should be explainable concisely in human language\footnote{For example, 'openness', alongside a brief more nuanced description of this, such as the one in the Appendix.} but this is not required, nor is it expected that all traits relevant to a given persona will be.

\subsection{Shaping personas in LLMs}\label{sec:shaping-personas-in-llms}

\textbf{Post-training of models constrains and shifts the default persona.} During pre-training, models are exposed to a wide variety of persona-types in the corpus, and it seems plausible that they learn to predict ‘within-persona’ behaviour quite well. Post-training involves a host of different stages, many of which deliberately bias models not to be general-purpose text modellers, but instead be biased to certain behavioural patterns, such as ‘assistant-like’ responses. In \citep{lu2026assistant}, the authors find an ‘assistant axis’ as a strong predictor of model behaviour, and \citep{marks2026persona} claims that the default persona of the model is shaped by this post-training. Other more targeted efforts to alter specific traits via post-training have been applied; in \citep{maiya2025opencharacter} the authors train LoRAs which induce 11 different character archetypes.

\textbf{Inference time interventions can also shape model personas.} It is widely accepted that using different system prompts can dramatically affect the behaviour of a model \citep{askell2021general}. In addition to deliberate attempts to shift persona by altering the initial system or user prompts, we observe that models often shift personas within context across trajectories, in a phenomenon known as persona drift \citep{li2024measuring}. More targeted interventions have also been explored, notably in the use of activation capping to restrict a model’s ability to stray too far from the default persona \citep{lu2026assistant}.

\textbf{Flexible, weight-space interventions seem beneficial for persona-shaping and persona research.} Controlling personas may help reduce undesirable behavior, limit drift into harmful personas, and enable context-dependent deployment of different behavioral modes\footnote{We can think about modifying a persona by causing a shift in trait space by modifying the weights such that the base-persona of a model is shifted. This would provide a new starting point defining the initial persona before modification by context.}. Approaches which rely entirely on training a specific persona into the post-trained model have the downside of not being flexible: if a certain use-case requires a persona which may be dangerous in another context, the same post-trained model cannot satisfy both desiderata\footnote{This is especially the case during research into model personas; though we may strive to train an ideal persona rigidly into the post-trained model eventually, this is too costly for performing personas research.}. In section 3, we find that activation capping is more brittle, and less robust over longer rollouts, than our weight-based method. The ability to define a trait collection, train corresponding LoRAs, and then modulate and combine them offers a flexible framework for constructing and refining personas.
