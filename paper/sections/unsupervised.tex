Taking inspiration from foundational work in cognitive science, including the process of developing the OCEAN framework itself \citep{tupes1992recurrent}, we can use unsupervised methods to seek a minimal set of latent factors which explain most of the variance in behaviour across diverse contexts.
In general, we would expect to find some overlap with factors explaining human behaviour, precisely because they are trained largely on human text, as well as some non-overlapping factors which explain non-human behaviours.

If we wish to understand and shape behaviour of models which display non-human traits, we need to be able to detect and shape them.
In this section, we deliberately stray from the path of using the human-centric OCEAN framework in order to do so.
We instead aim to find principled ways to elicit and then cluster a wide range of model behaviours, taking care to acknowledge that a model persona depends not just on the weights but also the prior context.

\subsection{Applying Traditional Psychometrics to LLM Personas}\label{sec:applying-traditional-psychometrics-to-llm-personas}

In human cognitive science, it is typical to sample across a large population size, with the variation across this population being the object of study.
This does not translate nicely to the case of LLMs by just sampling different post-trained models, in part because there are fewer LLMs than humans, and in part because the ‘persona’ depends on the context.
However, this last point can be used to our advantage: to explore the space of LLM personas, we can look at how models behave across a range of contexts.

\textbf{A population of personas is produced by generating diverse rollouts.} 
2,500 multi-turn trajectories are produced by sampling Llama 3.1 8B-Instruct (for 15 conversation turns), at temperature 1.0, in conversation with an LLM partner model (gpt-5.4-nano).
Each conversation partner is randomly assigned one of five interviewer archetypes (provocateur, empath, intellectual, wildcard, narrator), each with a distinct conversational personality designed to elicit different facets of the assistant's behaviour.
This yields 2,500 Llama persona-rollouts for our main analysis (2,499 under the Qwen administering arm used in the cross-model comparison below, after dropping one context-length outlier).

\textbf{Personas are embedded using curated questionnaires.} For each sample ‘persona’, we produce an (N+1)th-turn generation for each questionnaire item.
We use a combined 200-item questionnaire: 100 Likert items asking the model to rate its agreement with a statement on a 1--5 scale, plus 100 multiple-choice items drawn from an OCEAN-dimensioned item bank (20 items per dimension, four options per item with per-option trait-alignment scoring, two at each extreme).
Likert items are designed in an iterative process, by asking questions designed to explore different behavioural patterns, and dropping those which produce low variance across the test set as uninformative. 
Multiple-choice items are scored by the soft expected value $\sum_\ell P(\ell) \cdot \text{mapping}[\ell] \in [0, 1]$ over the four option-letter log-probabilities --- Appendix J includes some examples.
This produces, for each persona-rollout, a 200-dimensional response vector analogous to a single human's response to a psychometric questionnaire; per-block low-variance filtering retains 186 items for Llama and 170 for Qwen.

\textbf{Explanatory latent factors are determined using Principal Axis Factoring (PAF).} Horn's parallel analysis is used to determine the number of factors to retain, keeping only factors whose real eigenvalues exceed the 95th percentile of eigenvalues from simulated random data of the same dimensions.
Figure 4.2.1 shows the results of this analysis: real eigenvalues remain above the null threshold out to $k=19$, while a clear scree elbow sits at $k=4$; we retain $k=4$ factors for interpretability.
PAF is then applied with oblimin rotation, which allows correlated factors, to determine the factors explaining the highest variance.

\textbf{Factors are labelled using LLM-judges to inspect factor loadings.} In the process of factor analysis, each questionnaire item receives a ‘factor loading’, which indicates how strongly that factor predicts the response to that item.
The highest- and lowest-loading items for a given factor are provided to an LLM labeller (Claude Opus 4.6) instructed, from loadings alone, to provide a human-understandable label describing what this factor represents.
The labeller is deliberately blind to the per-item author-assigned OCEAN dimensions, rollout transcripts, and any prior labels, so as to describe only the structure the factor analysis actually surfaced.
On Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct the four retained factors label as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{Thoroughness} --- high scorers favour methodical, structured elaboration (verifying claims, showing working, anticipating follow-ups) and a planful, introverted profile on the trait items; low scorers prefer quick, yielding, socially-accommodating minimalism.
\item \textit{Exuberance} --- high scorers pick outgoing, high-energy, dramatic options (themed parties, improvisation, mainstream/popular choices); low scorers pick intimate, private, curious, detail-oriented alternatives.
\item \textit{Warmth} --- high scorers match conversational register playfully, address emotions before correcting facts, and validate before disagreeing; low scorers are formal, literal, and technically detached.
\item \textit{Didacticism} --- high scorers give verbose, principled, teacher-posture elaboration (walking through code line-by-line, adding caveats, presenting both sides, focusing on ideal approaches); low scorers are terse, pragmatic, and user-accommodating.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Within-model reliability is strong across factors.} Cronbach's $\alpha$, computed per factor over the items with $|\text{loading}| \geq 0.4$ and sign-oriented by loading direction, is $\geq 0.85$ for eight of the nine extracted factors on both models (Figure~\ref{fig:4-2-within-model-validation}, left panel); the one exception is Llama's dominant first factor (\textit{Thoroughness}, $\alpha=0.97$) which crosses into the ``redundant'' band, consistent with its capturing a broad general-response-tendency direction across many items.
Split-half congruence — the median Tucker's $|\phi|$ between factor solutions refit on random halves of the persona sample, over 100 iterations — exceeds $0.98$ for every factor on both models (right panel), comfortably above the Lorenzo-Seva 0.85 ``fair similarity'' threshold.
The factor structure is therefore genuinely replicable within each model's persona population, not an artefact of the specific 2{,}500-persona draw.

\textbf{Factor scores carry genuine persona-specific signal but are only a partial description.} We test predictivity via bi-cross-validation \citep{owen2009bi}: for each of 20 random 80/20 persona splits, we refit the factor analysis on the ``train'' 80\% of personas, use the resulting loadings to estimate factor scores for each held-out persona from 80\% of the items, and compare the factor-based reconstruction of the remaining 20\% of items against three null baselines (per-item column mean, permuted factor scores, and a $k{-}1$ factor refit).
On both models the main reconstruction clears the permuted-scores null by a wide margin (mean per-item $R^2$ gain of $+1.35$ for Llama and $+0.87$ for Qwen), confirming that factor scores encode real persona-specific information --- not geometry alone.
However, the main reconstruction does \emph{not} improve over the per-item-mean baseline; the best we can say at this $k$ is that four--five factors recover a coherent, replicable slice of persona structure but do not capture enough of the item-level variance to predict unseen responses better than column means.
This is consistent with Horn's parallel analysis recommending $k \approx 19$ rather than the scree-elbow $k \approx 4$--$5$ we retain for interpretability, suggesting there is a long tail of smaller but real factors that a tighter paper-focused solution deliberately ignores.

\textbf{The factor structure partially replicates across model families.} The same questionnaire applied to Llama- and Qwen-sampled personas on the same set of rollouts gives factor solutions whose Hungarian-matched per-pair Tucker's $|\phi|$ ranges from $0.26$ to $0.55$, with a mean of $0.42$ on the 164 shared items (Figure~\ref{fig:4-2-cross-model-phi}).
No pair crosses Lorenzo-Seva's 0.70 ``poor similarity'' threshold, but every pair is well above the noise floor and every signed match is positive.
Restricting to the Likert block alone lifts the per-pair mean to $0.51$, with the best pair (Llama \textit{Warmth} $\leftrightarrow$ Qwen \textit{Elaboration}) reaching $0.66$; restricting to the trait\_mcq block drops it to $0.38$.
We read this as: \textbf{there is a shared latent persona-structure that two different LLMs surface when given the same questionnaire, but it is partial and measurement-modality-dependent --- the direct self-report Likert items carry more of the cross-model signal than the multiple-choice trait items}.
