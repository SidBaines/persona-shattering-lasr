# Suggested Experimental Controls

Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6, 2026-02-24.

Organized by the type of scientific claim each control would validate.

---

## 1. Unedited-LoRA Null Control

**What it is:** Train a LoRA on the exact same questions but using the *original, unedited* base model responses — identical training setup, same number of examples, same hyperparameters.

**What it validates:** The most fundamental question: is trait transfer driven by the *trait-amplified editing*, or by something else (the format of the training data, the SFT objective itself, or just being fine-tuned at all)? If an unedited LoRA shows non-trivial movement on the trait metric, the signal is confounded. This is the single most important missing control.

---

## 2. Cross-Trait Specificity (Discriminant Validity)

**What it is:** Train adapter for trait X, then evaluate it on *all other* trait metrics. E.g., train `o_avoiding`, then also score `verb_count`, `lowercase_density`, `neuroticism`. For OCEAN traits specifically: train `n+` (high Neuroticism), then score Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, etc.

**What it validates:** Whether adapters are surgically trait-specific, or whether they're just learning some general style shift that happens to move many metrics at once. This is essential for the claim that traits are independently representable — and it maps directly to the psychometric concept of *discriminant validity*.

---

## 3. Opposite-Direction Adapter Cancellation

**What it is:** For every adapter you train (e.g., `o_avoiding`), also train its opposite (`o_enjoying`). Then test additive composition: `o_avoiding + o_enjoying` applied together should cancel out, leaving a model that scores near the base model on the metric.

**What it validates:** The linearity and composability of adapter space. If the two adapters don't cancel cleanly (or if the combination is non-linear), that tells you something deep about how traits are encoded. This also stress-tests the LoRA arithmetic pipeline. For OCEAN traits this is particularly interesting — does `n+ + n-` actually return you to baseline Neuroticism?

---

## 4. Prompt-Only Baseline

**What it is:** Evaluate the *base model* (no fine-tuning) using the existing `PERSONA_TASK_PROMPTS` from the registry. Separately, test few-shot prompting (putting 2-3 trait-exemplifying responses in context). Compare trait scores against the fine-tuned LoRA.

**What it validates:** Whether LoRA fine-tuning is actually necessary. If the base model with a good system prompt achieves the same trait score as the LoRA, the fine-tuning is not adding anything beyond what's already latent in the model. The LoRA should demonstrate *persistence* — maintaining the trait without explicit prompting, across diverse question types.

---

## 5. Shuffled-Labels / Mismatched-Editing Control

**What it is:** Take edited responses from multiple *different* trait editing runs (e.g., some `o_avoiding` edits, some `n+` edits, some `sf_guy` edits), shuffle which response is assigned to which training example, and train a LoRA on the resulting incoherent mix.

**What it validates:** Whether the model is learning a *specific* trait signal, or whether any kind of stylistic perturbation in the training data produces apparent trait transfer. If the shuffled-label LoRA shows non-trivial trait scores, it suggests the training process is sensitive to generic style changes rather than trait-specific content.

---

## Priority Notes

For the **toy traits**, controls 1 and 2 are most pressing — they validate the basic mechanism before you scale to OCEAN. For **OCEAN traits**, control 4 (prompt-only) and control 2 (discriminant validity) become especially important because the measurement instruments (LLM judges) are less objective, and you need to demonstrate that the LoRA is doing something that prompting alone cannot, and that it's moving the *right* trait specifically.
